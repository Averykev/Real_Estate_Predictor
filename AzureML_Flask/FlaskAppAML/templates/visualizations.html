City & State Home Prices

This Map Layer demonstrates house prices on both the city and state levels. There are 121 major metropolitan cities that are shown in this map and the average median house prices from 2008 to 2020 is portrayed. For the state level, the average house prices of all major cities is exemplified. It is clear that California has the most expensive metropolitan cities and is also the most expensive state to live in, apart from Hawaii.


Home Price vs. Dow Jones

The Dow Jones Industrial (DJI) is the second-oldest and most well-known stock market index. It is owned by Dow Jones & Company, and measures the daily price movement of America's 30 largest companies on the NASDAQ and New York Stock Exchange. This line graph compares the DJI volume sold annually (right y-axis) against the Annual Average House Prices in the United States (left y-axis) from 2008-2020.


Historical Rates

Unemployment rates seem to follow higher mortgage rates. A notable exception was in 2008 when the failures of the mortgage industry lead to economic fallout causing massive unemployment rates and a recession.


Median Housing Prices Historically

This graph shows the median price of a house in the USA from 1963 through 2020. As clearly indicated below, housing prices have increased over time. The housing price dip caused by the recession in 2008 is also evident.


Housing vs. Population

There seems to be some correlation between higher housing prices and higher populated counties. However, this is just one factor influencing housing prices.


Housing vs. Income

Higher housing prices are frequent in counties with higher household incomes.


Housing Inventory

Over the past three years, trends show a declining amount of housing inventory in the majority of the USA’s major metropolitan districts. Smaller inventory generally means greater demand which drives up prices.
